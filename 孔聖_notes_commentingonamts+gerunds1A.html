📊 COMMENTING ON AMOUNTS

enough / not enough · too much / too many · too few / too little

🎯 GRAMMATIC RULES: These structures express whether the quantity of something is sufficient or excessive.

• enough + noun (sufficient amount): We have enough time.
• not enough + noun (insufficient): There is not enough sugar.
• too much + uncountable noun (excessive amount): too much noise / too much water
• too many + countable plural noun (excessive number): too many people / too many cars
• too few + countable plural noun (insufficient number): too few applicants
• too little + uncountable noun (insufficient amount): too little information
• adjective + enough: She is old enough to drive.
• enough + noun vs adjective + enough – position matters.
💡 Model examples: “There are too many tourists in summer.” | “I have too little time to finish.” | “She didn't study enough.” | “He earned enough money to buy a car.” | “Too few students passed the exam.”

📝 GERUNDS

verb + -ing functioning as a noun · after prepositions · as subject/object

🎯 GRAMMATIC RULES: A gerund is the -ing form of a verb used as a noun. It can act as a subject, object, or come after prepositions and certain verbs.

• As subject: Swimming is good exercise.
• After prepositions: She is interested in learning French.
• After certain verbs (enjoy, avoid, mind, suggest, etc.): I enjoy reading novels.
• After possessive adjectives: Do you mind my opening the window?
• After expressions: It's no use crying over spilt milk. / There's no point in waiting.
• Gerund vs Infinitive: Some verbs are followed only by gerunds (admit, appreciate, avoid, consider, delay, deny, dislike, enjoy, finish, imagine, keep, mind, miss, practice, quit, recall, recommend, resist, risk, suggest).
💡 Model examples: “Traveling broadens the mind.” | “She avoided answering the question.” | “They apologized for being late.” | “I look forward to seeing you.”
