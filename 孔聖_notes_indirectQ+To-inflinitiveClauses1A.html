❓ INDIRECT QUESTIONS

embedded questions · polite inquiries · word order changes · if/whether

🎯 GRAMMATIC RULES: Indirect questions are questions embedded within a statement or another question. They are more formal and polite than direct questions.

• Word order: In indirect questions, use statement word order (subject + verb), NOT inversion.
• Introductory phrases: Could you tell me... / Do you know... / I wonder... / I'd like to know... / Can you explain...
• Yes/No questions: Use if or whether to introduce the embedded question.
• Wh- questions: Keep the wh- word (what, where, when, why, how) followed by subject + verb.
• No question mark when the indirect question is part of a statement (e.g., I wonder where she is.)

✅ Direct: Where is the station? → Indirect: Could you tell me where the station is?
✅ Direct: Does she speak French? → Indirect: I wonder if she speaks French.
💡 Model examples: “Do you know what time the meeting starts?” | “I'd like to know whether you have received my email.” | “Can you explain how this machine works?”

🎯 TO-INFINITIVE CLAUSES

infinitive of purpose · after certain verbs · as subject · adjective + to-infinitive

🎯 GRAMMATIC RULES: A to-infinitive clause consists of to + base verb and can function as a noun, adjective, or adverb. It expresses purpose, result, or follows specific patterns.

• Purpose (in order to): She left early to catch the train.
• After certain verbs (want, need, decide, hope, promise, agree, refuse, etc.): He agreed to help.
• As subject of sentence: To travel is a wonderful experience. (often with dummy 'it': It's important to arrive on time.)
• After adjectives: It's difficult to understand. / She was happy to see him.
• After question words (how, what, where, etc.): I don't know what to do.
• After too/enough: He's too young to drive. / She's old enough to vote.
• To express result: He woke up to find the door open.
💡 Model examples: “I called to confirm the reservation.” | “It's essential to arrive early.” | “She was the first to arrive.” | “He has a lot of work to do.”
